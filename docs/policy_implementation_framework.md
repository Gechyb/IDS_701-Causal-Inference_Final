# Policy Implementation and Monitoring Framework

## Purpose

This document translates the project's empirical findings into a practical monitoring framework for policymakers, labor agencies, workforce boards, employers, and local economic development organizations.

The main report finds that minimum wage increases are associated with clear wage gains in low-wage sectors, while aggregate employment effects in food services are small and statistically insignificant. However, the CPS heterogeneity analysis suggests that young adults aged 20 to 24 may face more employment pressure than other demographic groups. For that reason, the recommended policy approach is not simply to proceed or stop, but to proceed with targeted monitoring and support.

## Stakeholder-Specific Actions

| Stakeholder                           | Recommended action                                                                                                       | Indicators to monitor                                                                |
| ------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| State labor agencies                  | Continue scheduled wage increases while monitoring border-county labor outcomes                                          | Food service employment, average weekly wages, wage gaps across state borders        |
| Local workforce boards                | Prioritize young adults aged 20–24 for job placement and short-term training                                            | Young-adult employment rate, entry-level job postings, training enrollment           |
| Employers in food services and retail | Plan for wage increases through retention, scheduling, and productivity adjustments rather than immediate headcount cuts | Turnover, hours worked, vacancies, payroll costs                                     |
| Local economic development offices    | Use border-county comparisons to identify areas with unusual employment changes                                          | Business openings and closures, county employment trends, sector-specific employment |
| Worker advocates                      | Support wage increases while also advocating for programs that reduce entry-level employment barriers                    | Youth employment, wage growth, access to training and placement services             |

## Monitoring Logic

The border-county design is useful not only as a research method, but also as a practical monitoring tool. Instead of comparing a county only to the statewide average, policymakers can compare labor-market outcomes in counties located across state borders. This helps account for shared local demand, commuting patterns, and regional economic conditions.

A basic monitoring cycle could follow four steps:

1. Track food service employment and average weekly wages before and after a scheduled minimum wage increase.
2. Compare high-wage border counties with neighboring lower-wage counties.
3. Separately monitor young adults aged 20 to 24 because this subgroup shows the clearest negative employment estimate in the project.
4. Trigger targeted workforce support if young-adult employment weakens relative to neighboring counties for multiple periods.

## Suggested Trigger Points

The following trigger points are intended as practical early-warning signals rather than strict causal rules.

| Signal                                                                         | Interpretation                                         | Recommended response                                                        |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Wages rise and employment remains stable                                       | Policy is functioning as intended                      | Continue monitoring                                                         |
| Wages rise but young-adult employment falls relative to nearby border counties | Possible entry-level employment pressure               | Expand job placement, short-term training, or hiring incentives             |
| Wages rise but total food service employment falls for multiple periods        | Possible sector-level adjustment or local demand shock | Investigate local business conditions and compare with neighboring counties |
| No wage increase is observed after the policy change                           | Weak pass-through or data quality issue                | Review compliance, reporting, and sector coverage                           |
| Employment changes are concentrated in one region or industry                  | Possible geographic or sector-specific heterogeneity   | Conduct local follow-up analysis                                            |

## Interpretation Guidance

The project should not be interpreted as proof that minimum wage increases have no costs for any group. The more defensible conclusion is that the evidence does not show broad employment losses in food services, while one subgroup, young adults aged 20 to 24, deserves closer monitoring.

This framework is designed to help stakeholders use the results responsibly. It preserves the main policy implication that wage increases can raise pay without large aggregate job losses, while also recognizing uncertainty in the causal design and the need for targeted support.
